**Document Class:** HPF phenomenology subject / research invitation

## Abstract

This note presents a phenomenology subject within HPF: the possibility that regulator availability admits a dimensional closure threshold, such that 3D is the highest closure-stable class and 4D or higher is a closure-failing class under supersaturation. In this reading, the Big Bang for 3D may be interpreted as the collapse of a higher-dimensional parent regime into lawful 3D space. The note does **not** claim a closed derivation of the original boot sequence or of higher-dimensional ontology. Instead, it identifies a portable HPF object, traces its dimensional ladder through existing 1D, 2D, and 3D structures, and frames a set of open problems for further work.

The central portable object is regulator availability, denoted here by $a$, with the 3D vacuum-closure output written as $a_{\mathrm{vac}}$. The observed ladder is:

$$
a_1 \rightarrow a_2 \rightarrow a_3 \rightarrow a_{\mathrm{vac}}, \qquad a_4\;?
$$

where:

- $a_1$ = local regulator availability in 1D,
- $a_2$ = field-like regulator availability in 2D,
- $a_3$ = closure-capable regulator availability in 3D,
- $a_{\mathrm{vac}}$ = stable vacuum-sector closure output in 3D,
- $a_4?$ = hypothesized closure-failing availability class in 4D.

The point of this note is not to claim that 4D has been solved, but to mark a serious HPF research subject: whether 3D is special because regulator availability first closes stably there.

> Does regulator availability admit a dimensional closure threshold such that 3D is the first or highest closure-stable renormalizing class?

If yes, then 4D collapse may be a lawful **structural ancestry hypothesis** for 3D spacetime, even if 4D itself remains inaccessible to direct description.

## 2. Why this subject exists

Earlier HPF development used stronger projection and resolution language. As the framework matured, its center of gravity shifted toward:

- saturation,
- entropy phase,
- legality,
- routing,
- handoff,
- renormalization,
- recovery versus terminal failure.

That shift matters. The present note belongs to this later HPF posture.

The key question is no longer merely:

> How does bounded resolution produce projection effects?

The stronger question is:

> What objects remain lawful under supersaturation, and what dimensional classes can or cannot renormalize that load stably?

The dimensional-closure subject grows out of that second question.

The research program needs one object that can be traced across dimensions without being contaminated by post-forge 3D identities such as space-time conversion locks.

That object is regulator availability, written here as $a$.

Why $a$ is the right starting point:

- it is portable across dimensional reductions,
- it is not defined by the 3D lock between spatial interval and update interval,

- it provides a plausible dimensional failure criterion in 4D.

This makes $a$ the best candidate for bottom-up dimensional comparison.

## 4. The dimensional ladder

### 4.1 1D: local regulator availability

In 1D, the regulator object appears in its simplest form.

Interpretively, $a_1$ represents:

- local throughput permission,
- local drag or availability,
- minimal routing burden,
- line-based coherence control.

The 1D case is useful because it strips the system down to ordered extension. It is too simple to bear the full burden of higher-dimensional closure, but it provides the first recognizable regulator object.

### 4.2 2D: field-like regulator availability

In 2D, the regulator object becomes field-like rather than purely local.

Interpretively, $a_2$ represents:

- a regulator field over a sheet,
- coupling between transport and roughness,
- degradation and healing under geometric burden,
- nontrivial local-to-global interaction.

This is the first level where regulator availability begins to look like a true field rather than a gate bit.

### 4.3 3D: closure-capable regulator availability

In 3D, the regulator object crosses a threshold: it is not only local and field-like, but also closure-capable.

Interpretively, $a_3$ represents:

- volumetric regulator availability,
- stable renormalizing capacity,
- lawful closure under load,
- the first dimensional class capable of producing a stable vacuum-sector availability output.

This closure output is denoted:

$$
a_{\mathrm{vac}}
$$

The key phenomenological suggestion is that 3D is not special merely because it is the world we observe. It may be special because regulator availability first becomes globally closure-capable there.

### 4.4 4D: closure-failing candidate class

4D is not treated here as a solved domain. It is treated as a phenomenological target.

The hypothesis is that $a_4$ would be the first dimensional class in which regulator availability fails to close stably under supersaturation.

Interpretively, this means:

- too much independent freedom,
- too much routing burden,
- too much closure demand,
- insufficient terminal renormalization,
- or all of the above in combination.

Thus the ladder is not:

$$
1D < 2D < 3D < 4D
$$

in the sense of simple progressive richness.

It may instead be:

$$
1D \;\text{local} \rightarrow 2D \;\text{field-like} \rightarrow 3D \;\text{closure-stable} \rightarrow 4D \;\text{closure-failing}
$$

This is the hinge claim.

## 5. Big Bang interpretation under this subject

This note does **not** claim that 4D collapse explains absolute origin.

It proposes a more limited statement:

> 4D collapse may be the Big Bang **for 3D**.

This distinction matters.

The proposal is not that a higher-dimensional parent regime answers every origin question. It is that if a parent regime failed under supersaturation and collapsed into a closure-stable 3D class, then that collapse would be the boot event of our 3D spacetime.

This interpretation yields:

1. a parent regime becomes closure-failing,
2. collapse occurs,
3. lawful 3D is formed as the recovery class,
4. the resulting 3D sector begins oversaturated,
5. the 3D sector rings down through its saturation bands toward coherent geometry.

That is enough to reinterpret the Big Bang **for 3D** without pretending to narrate the native internal life of the parent regime.

## 7. Why the 4D parent cannot be described naively

A major discipline point follows from the hypothesis itself.

If 3D time is part of the forged outcome, then one cannot simply project 3D temporal intuitions backward and ask:

- how long did 4D exist,
- what happened one moment before collapse,
- what was the parent timeline like?

Those questions may be malformed from inside 3D.

So the correct posture is asymmetrical:

- we may infer **what 3D inherited** from the collapse,
- but we should not overclaim direct knowledge of what the parent regime was like on its own terms.

This keeps the subject controlled.

## 8. Relation to spacetime

The hypothesis does not require time to disappear.

A better reading is that in the forged 3D state, time ceases to be an independently free dimension in the old sense and becomes inseparable from ordered update and spatial interval through the mature regulatory structure.

So:

- space remains real,
- time remains real,
- but they are no longer independently loose.

This can be stated informally as:

> 3D did not lose time. It kept both space and time by forcing them into one regulated relation.

Or more sharply:

> 4D lost an independent freedom; 3D retained both space and time in fused regulated form.

This is one of the strongest intuitive consequences of the subject, even though it is not yet a proved theorem.
