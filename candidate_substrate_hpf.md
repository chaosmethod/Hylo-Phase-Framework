# Abstract

The Holographic Projection Framework (HPF) defines a regulatory architecture governing the lawful execution of physical theories under conditions of finite information resolution, bounded update bandwidth, and reversible microscopic dynamics. HPF itself does not assume a specific microscopic substrate.

However, when these regulatory constraints are applied systematically to candidate discrete substrates using the **HPF Theory Registry evaluation process**, certain structural properties become strongly favored. In particular, substrates must support reversible local update rules, lawful handling of congestion and saturation, local entanglement propagation, and approximate Lorentz symmetry in the infrared regime.

This note examines the class of discrete substrates consistent with these constraints and summarizes the results of registry-based evaluations. Among the candidates analyzed, a reversible body-centered cubic (BCC) bipartite lattice with a finite local register currently emerges as the **strongest tested executable substrate candidate**. The proposal is therefore presented as a **registry-derived working hypothesis**, rather than a postulated structure of spacetime.

# 1 Introduction

The Holographic Projection Framework (HPF) proposes that physical law execution is governed by a regulatory layer enforcing constraints on information flow, geometric stability, and entropy saturation.

Rather than introducing new fundamental forces or replacing established theories such as General Relativity (GR) or Quantum Field Theory (QFT), HPF defines conditions under which these theories remain valid and specifies routing mechanisms when their domain of applicability fails.

In this architecture:

```
Underlying substrate
        ↓
HPF regulatory layer
        ↓
Domain theories (GR, QFT, UHET, etc.)
```

HPF therefore constrains the class of possible microscopic substrates while remaining **agnostic about the specific implementation**.

To systematically evaluate candidate models, an **HPF Theory Registry agent** is used. The registry does not determine whether a theory is correct; instead it determines whether a theory or model is **legally executable under HPF constraints**.

The purpose of this note is to summarize the substrate candidates that emerge from this evaluation process.

# 2 Substrate Constraints Implied by HPF

When HPF legality conditions are enforced, any admissible substrate must satisfy several requirements.

### Finite local state space

Each site must possess a finite Hilbert space. Infinite precision degrees of freedom are incompatible with HPF resolution limits.

### Reversible microscopic dynamics

Microscopic updates must be invertible. Information deletion is prohibited.

### Local update rules

Evolution must occur through bounded local interactions.

### Entropy saturation handling

The substrate must support lawful redistribution of information when local update bandwidth approaches saturation.

### Entanglement propagation

Local unitary evolution must allow the generation and propagation of entangled states.

### Emergent Lorentz symmetry

The substrate must recover approximate Lorentz invariance in the infrared limit.

These constraints significantly narrow the class of viable substrates.

# 3 Registry Evaluation Process

Candidate substrates are evaluated using the **HPF Theory Registry**, which applies the HPF legality criteria to proposed models.

The registry evaluates whether a candidate model:

• defines a finite local state space
• provides reversible update rules
• exposes operational observables
• includes lawful handling of saturation and congestion
• defines explicit failure and routing conditions

The registry does **not determine physical truth**. Instead, it determines whether a model qualifies as a **legal executable expert** within the HPF–MDEA architecture.

Candidate substrates that pass the legality gates can then be ranked according to operational robustness, symmetry properties, and saturation resilience.

# 4 Candidate Lattice Families

Several discrete lattices have been examined under the registry evaluation process.

### Cubic lattice

The cubic lattice provides a simple reversible substrate with coordination number 6. It serves as a useful control architecture but tends to exhibit stronger anisotropy and reduced transport capacity under saturation.

### Hyperdiamond lattice

The hyperdiamond lattice possesses elegant tetrahedral transport directions and appears naturally in several discrete relativistic models. However, its coordination number of 4 limits throughput under congestion conditions.

### Body-centered cubic lattice

The body-centered cubic (BCC) lattice has coordination number 8 and a natural bipartite structure. This combination provides high transport capacity, improved isotropy, and convenient partitioning for reversible update schedules.

When evaluated through the HPF Theory Registry process, the BCC architecture has so far satisfied the legality conditions most cleanly and shown the strongest operational robustness among tested candidates.

# 5 Bipartite Structure

The BCC lattice can be partitioned into two disjoint sublattices:

```
A-sublattice
B-sublattice
```

All nearest-neighbor edges connect sites from one sublattice to the other.

This bipartite structure enables reversible staggered update schedules:

```
A → B streaming
B → A streaming
```

Such update patterns are widely used in reversible cellular automata and quantum walk constructions.

# 7 Reversible Update Operators

A minimal reversible update sequence may consist of four operators.

```
U_block = U_relax U_mix U_stream U_ren
```

### Regulator update

Local congestion and entropy pressure update the regulator state controlling update availability.

### Transport operator

Spinor amplitudes propagate between nearest neighbors on the lattice.

### Local mixing operator

Internal spinor degrees of freedom mix locally, producing interference and effective mass terms.

### Redistribution operator

Under saturation conditions, excess load is temporarily transferred into reversible storage and redistributed locally.

# 8 Entropy-Phase Behavior

HPF predicts several execution regimes depending on entropy pressure.

Low entropy regimes produce smooth propagation and emergent geometric behavior consistent with GR-like dynamics.

Intermediate regimes produce strong quantum interference.

As entropy pressure increases, redistribution mechanisms activate to prevent illegal information deletion.

Under extreme saturation, routing to ultra-high-energy execution regimes occurs.

A substrate capable of hosting all of these behaviors must therefore support both coherent propagation and lawful congestion handling.
